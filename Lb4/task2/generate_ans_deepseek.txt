That's a great question, and scientists don't have a single, definitive answer, as the exact reason is still being researched. However, several compelling theories exist:

1. **Camouflage (Motion Displacement):** This is the most commonly cited reason.

	*    The black and white stripes create a confusing pattern that makes it hard for predators (like lions) to clearly see a zebra's outline or determine its direction of movement when viewed from certain angles, especially in the tall grass.

	*    The stripes might break up the zebra's outline, making it blend in with the dappled light and shadow of its surroundings or confusing the predator's vision. Some think the high contrast creates an optical illusion of motion that makes the zebra harder to target.

2. **Herd Formation (Herd Effect):** This theory suggests stripes help zebras move together in a group

	*    When zebras stand close together, the black stripes of one and the white stripes of the next adjacent zebra form larger, confusing shapes for predators. This herd pattern itself acts as camouflage, breaking up the outline of the entire group.

3. **Thermal Regulation (Cooler Air):** This theory proposes that the black and white stripes help regulate body temperature.

	*    Black absorbs heat, white reflects it. It's theorized that the black stripes heat up slightly more than the white stripes when exposed to sunlight.

	*    This temperature difference creates small convection currents (warm air rising from the black stripes, cool air flowing towards them from the white stripes). These tiny currents might help cool the zebra's body more effectively than a uniform color would.

4. **Insect Repellent:** This is a more recent hypothesis

	*    Some researchers noticed that certain biting flies (like tsetse flies) seem less attracted to striped patterns than to solid colors. The idea is that the black and white stripes create a confusing visual signal or perhaps generate air currents that deter the flies.

	*    While intriguing, this theory is still being investigated and debated, as the evidence isn't conclusive.
