The exact reason why zebras have black stripes is still a subject of scientific debate. Biologists believe it is not just one single cause, but likely a combination of several evolutionary advantages.

Here are the four leading theories regarding why zebras have their distinctive striped patterns:

### 1. Protection from Insects (The "Bug Defense" Theory)

This is currently considered one of the strongest theories.

**Tsetse Flies:** Zebras live in areas where Tsetse flies and ticks are common. These insects can transmit diseases like sleeping sickness to livestock.

**Visual Disruption:** Research suggests that the stripes disrupt the visual cues insects use to find a host. The high-contrast pattern makes it difficult for flies to lock onto the zebra, causing them to overshoot or hesitate.

**Evidence:** Studies have shown that tick-infested zebras have significantly fewer ticks on their striped bodies compared to horses or donkeys (which lack stripes).

### 2. Predator Confusion (Motion Dazzle)

**Blood Vessel Protection:** Some studies suggest that the stripes disrupt blood vessel patterns on the skin, which could help prevent insects from landing on sensitive areas or regulate heat dissipation in specific spots.



### 3. Social Camouflage (Wrinkle Theory)

This theory focuses on the zebras' own behavior rather than external threats.

**Group Cohesion:** In a large herd, individual zebras might have trouble recognizing each other. The unique stripe pattern acts like a fingerprint, allowing them to identify herd members and stay together.

**Blending in:** While they don't hide perfectly in the grass, the stripes may help them blend into the dappled light of the tall grass and tree shadows at dawn and dusk.

### Summary

While there is no single "silver bullet" answer, most scientists believe that **insect protection** and **predator confusion** are the primary drivers for the black stripes. The stripes are a complex evolutionary adaptation that likely solves multiple problems at once.

**Note:** The stripes are actually a "negative image" in terms of genetics. Scientists believe zebras evolved with black pigmentation and white "holes" (white is the default lack of pigment in hair). As they evolved, they began to "fill in" the coat with black pigment in a specific pattern.
