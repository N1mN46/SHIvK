The exact reason for zebra stripes is still a subject of scientific research, and while several theories exist, the most widely accepted explanation involves a combination of factors, primarily camouflage and possibly insect repellence.

Here are the leading theories:

1. **Camouflage:** This is the most popular theory. The black-and-white pattern breaks up the zebra's outline, making it harder for predators (like lions or hyenas) to single out the zebra in the tall grass or scrubland. The stripes create a sort of "motion camouflage" or confuse predators trying to judge dis tance or isolate the animal from the background. Some studies suggest the pattern provides better camouflage than solid black or white stripes.

2. **Social Recognition:** Zebras are highly social animals that live in herds. Each individual has a unique stripe pattern, similar to human fingerprints. This distinctiveness likely helps zebras recognize each other, especially in large groups or when mothers need to identify their foals. The contrast betwe en the stripes might also help them see each other clearly in dusty or low-light conditions.

3. **Insect Repellent:** This theory suggests the black and white stripes are effective against biting flies, such as tsetse flies or horseflies. The high contrast pattern might confuse insects that use visual cues to find a host. Research (e.g., from the University of Bristol) using computer models has shown that certain stripe patterns can deter flies more effectively than solid colors. Flies might be fooled into thinking they've reached a solid color (black or white) by the time they get close, or the pattern creates turbulence that discourages landing.

4. **Thermoregulation:** Some speculate that the stripes might aid in cooling. Black areas absorb heat, while white reflects it. As a zebra moves, the stripes might create small pockets of air movement or varying temperatures across its body, potentially helping with heat dissipation. However, this theory is

considered less significant than camouflage or insect repellence.

**Conclusion:**

It's likely that zebras evolved their distinctive stripes through a combination of these factors. The camouflage provided an advantage against predators, while the unique patterns aided social structure. The insect repellent effect would have been a significant bonus, reducing the annoyance and potential disea se transmission from biting flies. Natural selection would have favored zebras with patterns that offered the best survival advantage in their specific environment.
